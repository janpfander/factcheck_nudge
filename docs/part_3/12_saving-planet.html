Clean air act and energy consumption

By: Teresa

‚ÄúMany of their actions have done a lot of good; efforts to reduce air pollution have prevented hundreds of thousands of premature deaths and millions of illnesses as well. ‚Äú In the United States, national emissions limitations imposed on major pollution sources have been the rule, not the exception. Such limitations have sometimes been effective; the air is much cleaner than it was in 1970.‚Äù (p437)

Context: This chapter examines government efforts to protect the environment, focusing on strict regulations, particularly in the United States, where national emissions limitations have been imposed on major pollution sources. These measures have significantly reduced air pollution since 1970.

  • Indeed, in the United States, a ‚ÄúClean Air Act‚Äù was agreed in 1970 and allows the reduction in particulate air pollution alone. In the same year that saw the founding of Earth Day and the creation of the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), President Nixon signed into law the Clean Air Act of 1970 (CAA) on December 31, ushering in what is arguably the most important and far-reaching environmental statute enacted in the United States. [https://www.resources.org/archives/looking-back-50-years-clean-air-act-1970 ]. This act passed by the congress established the National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS), setting maximum allowable concentrations of particulate matter, among other pollutants, created emissions standards for pollution sources, leading industrial facilities to install pollution control technologies and automakers to produce cleaner, more fuel-efficient vehicle and required each state government to devise its own plan for achieving and sustaining compliance with the standards[https://www.unep.org/news-and-stories/story/united-states-clean-air-act-turns-50-air-any-better-half-century-later; https://www.epa.gov/sites/default/files/2015-06/documents/contsetc.pdf]
  • By 1980, control of industrial emissions had led to a 50% decrease in particulate emissions, or a 20% decrease in ambient PM concentrations across the country. Today, on average, the PM2.5 pollution that Americans are exposed to is only 35.1 percent of what it was in 1970. [https://aqli.epic.uchicago.edu/policy-impacts/united-states-clean-air-act/#:~:text=With%2064.9%20percent%20less%20pollution,the%20average%20American%20since%201970.]
  • Clean Air Act programs have lowered levels of six common pollutants (particles, ozone, lead, carbon monoxide, nitrogen dioxide and sulfur dioxide) as well as numerous toxic pollutants.
  • Between 1990 and 2020, national concentrations of air pollutants improved 73 percent for carbon monoxide, 86 percent for lead (from 2010), 61 percent for annual nitrogen dioxide, 25 percent for ozone, 26 percent for 24-hour coarse particle concentrations, 41 percent for annual fine particles (from 2000), and 91 percent for sulfur dioxide. [epa.gov] [https://www.epa.gov/clean-air-act-overview/air-pollution-current-and-future-challenge]
  • Indeed, efforts to reduce air pollution have prevented hundreds of thousands of premature deaths and millions of illnesses as well. Now, with 64.9 percent less pollution in the air, Americans are living healthier, with longer lives : 1.4 years have been added to the life expectancy of the average American since 1970. [https://aqli.epic.uchicago.edu/policy-impacts/united-states-clean-air-act/#:~:text=With%2064.9%20percent%20less%20pollution,the%20average%20American%20since%201970.]

However, it‚Äôs important to highlight that even though the Clean Air Act has improved air quality, fifty years have passed since its introduction. Indeed, recent research has shown an increase in dangerous air pollution levels in recent years. Moreover, greenhouse gas emissions are now recognized as a danger to public health. Nevertheless, the United States still accounts for 13 percent of global greenhouse gases, and this percentage is still rising.

Are Disclosure Laws the Sole Reason for Reduced Toxic Emissions?

By: Micaela & Sofia

‚ÄúThe surprising fact is that without mandating any behavioral change, this law (the Emergency Planning and Community Right to Know Act) has had massive beneficial effects, spurring large reductions in toxic releases throughout the United States. This unanticipated consequence suggested that all by themselves, disclosure requirements might be able to produce significant emissions reductions.*‚Äù

A disclosure statement is a document where companies have to reveal certain information to the public or government. For environmental laws, this often means reporting the types and amounts of toxic chemicals they release into the environment. The idea is that if companies have to make this information public, they will work harder to reduce harmful emissions to avoid looking bad.

The author is talking about the Emergency Planning and Community Right to Know Act (EPCRA), a U.S. law from 1986. This law requires companies to report on how they store, use, and release hazardous substances. James Hamilton‚Äôs book talks about how just making this information public can lead to big reductions in toxic emissions because companies want to avoid bad publicity.

  • We fact-checked all references provided by the author as Hamilton, James. Regulation Through Revelation. New York: Cambridge University Press 2005 but we didn‚Äôt find this paper with free access on the internet.
  • The quote might oversimplify the mechanisms behind the reductions in toxic releases. While disclosure is a significant factor, other elements such as advances in technology, economic shifts, regulatory pressures, and corporate social responsibility initiatives also play crucial roles in reducing emissions.
  • The impact of disclosure requirements can vary significantly across different industries or even different regions. Some companies might respond promptly to public disclosure, while others might not be as affected. The quote does not account for this variability.

Does raising taxes incites people to reduce their fuel consumption ?

By: Mayva & Julia

‚ÄúBut raising the tax on gasoline, for example, would eventually induce drivers to buy more fuel-efficient cars, drive less, or both. As a result, emissions of carbon dioxide, the leading contributor to global warming, would decline. And if gas taxes were increased, automobile manufacturers would have plenty of incentives to develop new technologies to meet the demand for more fuel-efficient cars.‚Äù

This book was written in 2008, so policies that increase taxes on gasoline have been tried in the world. The results are not always in line with what the authors claim: E.g. in their study ‚ÄùEffect of gasoline prices on car fuel efficiency: Evidence from Lebanon‚Äù, Walid Marrouch and Jana Mourad found no evidence that rising gasoline price shifted consumer‚Äôs demand toward more fuel-efficient cars.

Also, we can socially question such taxes. We can take the case of France to illustrate, where this raise in tax led to an important social movement: Yellow Jackets. The tax only affected the poor, who really needed their car to go work, and couldn‚Äôt afford to buy a new car. Indeed, gasoline is an inelastic good, so a rise in its price has a big social impact. In France, the protest was so high that the government finally had to go back and finally abandoned the tax.
